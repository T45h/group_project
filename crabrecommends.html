I recommend

As an intensely cultural crab, I feel it is my duty to impart some of my wisdom to you through a few reviews.

Ken Kiff: The Sequence • Sainsbury Centre, Norwich • 17th November – Sunday 21st April Shown in it’s closest proximity to completeness yet, the 60 piece series of paintings from Ken Kiff’s Sequence at the Sainsbury Centre is well worth the trip (and was also for me a wonderful excuse to discover the delights of Norwich). Despite his paintings are often unrealistic and illogical, to the point that on first glance they appear almost childish, fusing sensitive application of colour allows them to sing for themselves. The effect of joyful colour palette, combined with his strange, and often slightly unsavoury subject matter emphasises an arresting fusion of various juxtapositions the beautiful and ugly, the fantastical and everyday banality.

Jeff Koons at The Ashmolean • Oxford • 7th Feb – 9th June I went to this exhibition with reservation. The giant of the art money world, Jeff Koon’s works seemed to me to be nothing more than ginourmously oversized and garishly kitsch. However, as this exhibition reveals, as a conceptual artist Koons excels, and that actually the thinking behind what can at appear first glance as rather un-thought out, is interestingly extensive, and where he lacks a use of traditional skill, he certainly makes up for with attention to detail. It can’t be denied- there is some strange appeal to artwork which so directly appeals to our senses- an inflatable balloon the height of a bus and made of metal is delightful. Koons’ ability to create art which is in dialogue with art history, and which connects highly intelligent and philosophical ideas, and a very basic sensory enjoyment makes him perhaps one of the most democratic artists of our day.
Dorothea Tanning • Tate, London • 27 February – June 2019 Thank you Tate for re-shedding on this incredible artist, in her first large-scale exhibition of her work in over 25 years. Whilst Dorothy Tanning has perhaps lived slightly in the shadows of her husband and one giants of the Surrealist movement, Max Ernst, this exhibition shows Surrealism as taken to an entirely new level. Whilst older paintings maintain an intricately wacky gothic scenarios and landscapes which invite the viewer into a world beyond the tangible, her later paintings ask no questions. As epic in subject matter as they are in size, Tanning confronts us with what it is to be a body in a series of writhing paintings, convulsing with abstracted forms which are at once undoubtedly human, and unsettlingly alien. This sense of discomfort is enhance, as despite the sensuous fleshiness of the brushstrokes, it is difficult to tell whether these tangled masses of figures are revellers or rivals. The same could be said for her soft sculptures, which reduced to a heap of suggestively limb-like curves radiate a disquieting sense of vulnerability and helplessness. With her uncanny ability to evoke such emotion and tangibility through such an abstracted pose or gesture, Tanning leaves us with some disconcerting questions about what actually constitute life and perhaps what it means to take it.
Wildlife Photographer of the Year • Natural History Museum, London • On ‘til 30th Jun 2019 Whether you are a wildlife fanatic or not, there is so much to gain from this show. ‘Wildlife Photographer of the Year’ is a stunning exhibition, made more so by its considered combination of image with word. Photographs that are captivating in their own right are made all the more powerful by the stories written below, which give insight into the wildlife, and the phenomenal effort and length taken to capture each picture. Particularly poignant is Adrian Bliss’s fox, quiet in the midst of Chernobyl’s rubble, which since its human abandonment has become a thriving habitat for Ukrainian wildlife. Photo-journalism shots document still deeper stories; painful snapshots of exploitation show a tiger debilitated by poaching, a sun bear crying from its cage, a monkey trapped in a circus which continues to go on. These are interspersed with more hopeful portraits, like of Bob the flamingo spreading the message for conservation on national television, and soothing rescued flamingos to aid their rehabilitation. With each photograph, you see the power and the intricacy of nature, and are made to feel first hand the devastating effect that our impact has had on it. A lot is told and a lot can be learned. You come away with with a greater understanding of the animal world, and the time, skill and passion that goes into capturing it. There is a real sense of the passion behind the lens being shared, and I left feeling strangely uplifted, and enlivened by the thought of the opportunity that this exhibitions brings; for so many people to be made aware of these stories which are lesser known; to be moved by that which is not so often told; and hopefully to feel some connection, and desire to conserve these places from which they are so far removed. Pictured: detail of Dariío Podestá’s ‘Argentine Quickstep’
The Sun and Her Flowers • Rupi Kaur Rupi Kaur’s done it! She writes poems for everyone- even those that think they don’t like poetry! Beautifully framed within her central message that people must 'fall, root, rise, in order to bloom' Kaur’s collection movingly narrates a journey from grief and abjection to love and empowerment. Kaur combines simple illustrations with economically written passages, whose fragmentary nature resemble the thought patterns of their powerfully singular voice. Un-punctuated, uncapitalised and unassuming on the page, her words resound with a weighty rawness, confronting emotionally difficult traumas and setbacks with sensitivity and acceptance. Comfortingly honest and beautifully uplifting, this IS a must read.
In the Company of • TJ Boulting, London • Nov 2018 If you have a spare hour over the rest of the week, catch ‘In the company of’ before it ends on the 17th November. Curated by Katy Hessel of @thegreatwomenartists, the exhibition places work from three very established artists Alice Neel, Barbara Hepworth and Lee Miller, beside pieces by twelve contemporary female artists to highlight their influence upon, and affinities with their preceding generations. Hessel’s curation does well to cement the idea of the shared artistic language between the old and new: little priority is given to any artist, and pieces from established artists are interpsersed with that of the emerging; encouraging the sense of their being in dialogue with one another. All of these artists use traditional techniques in contemporary ways, and it’s great to see such a diverse range of mediums and styles complementing and contrasting with one another. Rather than leave too much to the imagination- an angle frequently adopted by contemporary galleries that is guilty for leaving an observer at a complete and utter loss- Hessel’s short descriptions and quotes from each contemporary artist give an insight into each work that is both informative and digestible. A worthwhile trip to see work from some really exciting new artists as well as from some old favourites that are usually stashed away in private collections. Pictured: detail from Jessie Makinson’s ‘Double Sign’

Ken Kiff: The Sequence • Sainsbury Centre, Norwich • 17th November – Sunday 21st April Shown in it’s closest proximity to completeness yet, the 60 piece series of paintings from Ken Kiff’s Sequence at the Sainsbury Centre is well worth the trip (and was also for me a wonderful excuse to discover the delights of Norwich). Despite his paintings are often unrealistic and illogical, to the point that on first glance they appear almost childish, fusing sensitive application of colour allows them to sing for themselves. The effect of joyful colour palette, combined with his strange, and often slightly unsavoury subject matter emphasises an arresting fusion of various juxtapositions –the beautiful and ugly, the fantastical and everyday banality. Kiff’s paintings brim with narratives, which manage to be both conflicting and harmonious. It is perhaps due to his ability to create such an imaginative space, so filled with the contrariness and arbitrariness of life that people find his work so appealing, and relatable. Whilst very revered in his day for bridging the gaps between Abstract Expressionism and Modernism, Kiff sadly seems to have slipped into obscurity in the public’s recent collective consciousness—all the more reason to see these works, many of which are in private collections, before they disappear again.
